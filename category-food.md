# Food — category standards (live-checked, EU-anchored, India-strict)

> **This file is a map, not a rulebook.** Food standards, limits **and legal
> statuses change** — this file says **what to check and where**; it does **not**
> freeze numbers or "mandatory/banned" statuses. Pull them live at query time.
>
> **Regulators here differ from cosmetics — and FSSAI *is* the right one now.**
> - **India: FSSAI** is the food authority (not a mistake here — food is its job).
>   Every pack must carry a **valid FSSAI licence / registration number** (14-digit)
>   — verify it on the **FoSCoS** portal. But a valid licence is evidence about the
>   **food business, not proof the product / batch / label is good** — cross-check
>   the business name, address, the manufacturer / marketer / importer role, the
>   product category where shown, and that the label identity matches. Plus FSSAI
>   standards + labelling regulations, and FSSAI recall / surveillance notices.
>   Quality marks (voluntary, and availability varies by commodity — **verify the
>   current status of each**): **AGMARK**, **Jaivik Bharat / India Organic (NPOP)**
>   for organic, the **veg (green) / non-veg (brown) dot.**
> - **EU / EFSA = the anchor for "strict."** EU food law is **often the tightest**
>   on **additives, contaminant maximums, and pesticide residues (MRLs)** — check the
>   current EU frameworks on **EUR-Lex** and the **EU pesticide-MRL database** rather
>   than hardcoding a regulation number or a limit. *Compare **per hazard**: the EU
>   isn't universally strictest for every substance, and a stricter limit can reflect
>   a policy choice, not only safety. Where another jurisdiction is stricter on a
>   specific hazard, use that.*
> - **US FDA** cross-check; **Codex Alimentarius** for international reference
>   (infant formula, contaminants).
>
> **India reality — read first.** The dominant risk here is **adulteration and
> residue exceedance**, not label additives: milk, edible oils, spices, and honey
> are the classic targets, and enforcement is uneven. So **"FSSAI-licensed" is the
> floor, not proof of quality** — prefer products with **current, batch-relevant
> test evidence** and good traceability, and be suspicious of too-cheap staples.
>
> **Classify first:** is it a **food**, a **nutraceutical / supplement** (FSSAI's
> nutraceutical regime), or is it making **disease / medical claims** (which may put
> it under drug or AYUSH regulation)? Flag the claim for classification rather than
> deciding from a single phrase.
>
> **What "good" means here has two parts, ranked by *Ranking* in `instructions.md`
> (safety first):**
> 1. **Safety & purity** — contamination, adulteration, residues, authenticity.
>    *Dominates for staples* (spice, oil, honey, water, flour).
> 2. **Nutritional quality & suitability** — added sugar, sodium, saturated / trans
>    fat, fibre, protein quality, degree of processing, fortification relevance,
>    allergen / dietary / medical fit, and taste-adherence. *Carries more weight for
>    everyday packaged foods* (cereal, snacks, spreads, yoghurt). A product can be
>    contaminant-safe and still be the wrong nutritional choice.
>
> **Evidence hierarchy:** valid FSSAI licence + current label → applicable standard
> (EU / FSSAI / Codex, fetched live) → **credible current, product-specific lab
> test** (third-party or FSSAI surveillance) → reviews **only** for taste / texture.

**Schema per sub-category:** What matters → Check live (where) → EU-vs-India gap →
Red flags → Cross-check (India marks / brands) → Review lens → Evidence.
**Generate candidates from criteria, not brand names** (cross-check, not a start).
Mark undisclosed decisive figures **"not publicly verifiable."**
**To add a category, copy this structure into `category-<name>.md`.**

## 1. Packaged & processed foods (snacks, ready-to-eat, spreads, cereals, sauces)
- **What matters** — **two lenses**: (a) additive / colour safety + honest
  labelling; (b) **nutritional quality** — added sugar, sodium, saturated / trans
  fat, fibre, protein quality, degree of processing, fortification, and fit for my
  dietary / medical context (e.g. diabetes, hypertension, an allergen).
- **Check live** — additives vs the **current EU additives framework** (the EU bans
  or warning-labels several colours / additives India still allows — e.g. certain azo
  dyes; verify which, live); FSSAI additive limits; the **current industrial trans-fat
  cap** (FSSAI caps it — check the figure); front-of-pack claims vs the ingredient
  list; the nutrition panel per 100 g **and** per realistic serving.
- **Nutrition lens** — read the ingredient order and per-100 g numbers, not the
  marketing; watch hidden sugars (syrups, concentrates, "-ose" names), sodium and
  ultra-processing. For a stated need ("best cereal for a diabetic"), suitability +
  added sugar + fibre lead — *after* safety, per the priority order.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — EU tends to restrict / label more additives and colours;
  prefer EU-compliant formulations, verified per additive against the live list.
- **Red flags** — illegal adulterant dyes (metanil yellow, rhodamine B, Sudan — these
  are classic banned food colours); "no preservatives" while clearly using them;
  missing / invalid FSSAI number; vague "natural / health / immunity" claims; "no
  added sugar" hiding sugar alcohols or juice concentrate.
- **Cross-check** — valid FSSAI licence on FoSCoS; independent tests; the actual
  nutrition panel.
- **Review lens** — taste / texture / adherence only.
- **Evidence** — EFSA, FSSAI, independent lab studies; nutrition science for the
  dietary context.

## 2. Edible oils & ghee
- **What matters** — purity (no adulteration / hidden blending), trans fat (no
  vanaspati passed as ghee), freshness / oxidation, extraction (cold-pressed claims).
- **Check live** — FSSAI oil standards; adulterant tests — **argemone oil in mustard
  oil** is a serious historical adulterant (caused epidemic dropsy); the current
  trans-fat cap; EU contaminant limits (3-MCPD / glycidyl esters, mineral-oil
  residues) as the stricter reference. **AGMARK's status for oils / ghee has been
  shifting toward voluntary — check the current requirement**; treat a verifiable
  grade mark as a bonus, don't penalise its absence.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — EU tends to be tighter on refining contaminants; prefer
  products with evidence against those where they matter.
- **Red flags** — suspiciously cheap "pure" oil or ghee; undeclared blending;
  vanaspati sold as ghee; loose unbranded oil of unknown source.
- **Cross-check** — verifiable grade / test evidence, sealed branded packs; a verified
  mill can be as good as a big brand — judge on traceability + testing, not packaging
  alone.
- **Evidence** — FSSAI, EFSA, independent tests.

## 3. Dairy & milk products
- **What matters** — adulteration, proper pasteurisation, contaminants, fat / SNF
  standards.
- **Check live** — FSSAI milk standards; classic adulterants (water, detergent, urea,
  starch, "synthetic milk"); **aflatoxin M1** limits (the EU limit is stricter —
  verify both).
- **Safety** — raw / unpasteurised milk and soft cheeses carry real infection risk,
  **especially in pregnancy, young children and the elderly** — prefer pasteurised.
- **Red flags** — loose milk of unknown source; detergent / soapy taste;
  unrefrigerated dairy; "too white" synthetic milk.
- **Cross-check** — pasteurised, cold-chain intact; a verified cooperative or dairy
  (e.g. Amul, Mother Dairy) can be as trustworthy as any brand — judge on traceability
  + testing, not "branded vs loose" alone.
- **Evidence** — FSSAI surveillance, independent tests.

## 4. Spices & condiments
- **What matters** — adulteration, contaminants (**aflatoxins, pesticide residue,
  ethylene oxide**), illegal colour dyes.
- **Check live** — FSSAI spice standards; **EU MRLs including ethylene oxide** —
  Indian spice brands have been recalled abroad over ETO / pesticide across multiple
  years, so check current **EU RASFF** and importing-country alerts; **lead chromate
  in turmeric** (documented colour adulteration — a serious lead-exposure source);
  Sudan dye / brick powder in chilli; papaya seeds in black pepper.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — EU ethylene-oxide and pesticide limits are far stricter →
  prefer products with **current, batch-relevant evidence against the EU limits**.
  ("Export quality" on the label with no documentation is marketing, not proof.)
- **Red flags** — unnaturally bright turmeric / chilli (dye or lead chromate); very
  cheap loose spices; **a current or recurring recall for the exact product** (check
  SKU / batch / date — one resolved historical recall isn't automatic disqualification,
  but a *pattern* is a serious flag).
- **Cross-check** — sealed branded, verifiable test / grade evidence.
- **Evidence** — FSSAI, EU RASFF alerts, published lead-in-turmeric research.

## 5. Honey & sweeteners
- **What matters** — sugar-syrup adulteration, genuine purity.
- **Check live** — FSSAI honey standard **plus the advanced tests** (NMR, C4 sugar,
  foreign-oligosaccharide / SMR) — a **2020 CSE study found several major Indian
  brands passed basic tests but failed the NMR test** for syrup adulteration. Treat
  that as **historical category-risk, not proof about today's batch** — brands and
  suppliers change; use current, product-specific test evidence for the verdict.
- **Red flags** — cheap "pure" honey; brands with recent adulteration findings.
  *(Crystallisation is **not** a reliable authenticity test — it depends on the
  glucose / fructose ratio, floral source, temperature and storage. Don't use "never
  crystallises" — or "crystallises fast" — as decisive evidence.)*
- **Cross-check** — current NMR / lab-tested; judge a producer on traceability and
  testing, **not** on being "small / local / natural" (small-scale can have weaker
  batch testing).
- **Evidence** — current lab / NMR reports; the CSE study for historical context only.

## 6. Grains, pulses, flours, nuts & dry fruit
- **What matters** — **aflatoxins / mycotoxins** (nuts, maize), pesticide residues,
  heavy metals, artificial polishing / coating (pulses), foreign matter.
- **Check live** — **EU aflatoxin and pesticide MRLs** (typically stricter — verify
  the current figures); FSSAI limits; coating agents on pulses (mineral oil /
  leather-dye polish is documented).
- **Safety** — aflatoxin is a liver toxin / carcinogen → **discard mouldy nuts or
  grains**, don't just clean them.
- **Red flags** — unnaturally glossy pulses; musty / mouldy nuts; very cheap "premium"
  dry fruit.
- **Cross-check** — lab-tested, organic (NPOP) where relevant; sealed branded reduces
  adulteration risk, but a verified mill / source can match — judge on traceability +
  testing.
- **Evidence** — FSSAI, EFSA, independent tests.

## 7. Beverages & packaged water
- **What matters** — packaged-water safety, soft-drink additives / residue, caffeine
  / energy-drink limits, added sugar.
- **Check live (this is a *verify-not-hardcode* area — the rules changed recently).**
  Packaged & mineral water certification in India **shifted away from a mandatory
  BIS/ISI mark toward an FSSAI testing-scheme approach**, so **check the *current*
  FSSAI licence + packaged-water testing / labelling requirement live** — **don't
  assume an ISI mark is (or isn't) mandatory, and don't reject a product solely for
  lacking one.** A *voluntary* BIS mark, where present, is still positive evidence.
  Also verify the current source / batch / test-record requirements. For soft drinks:
  FSSAI beverage standards + EU additive / sweetener status (historic: CSE found
  pesticide residues in soft drinks — check current data).
- **Red flags** — unlabeled / refill water of unknown source; missing FSSAI licence;
  energy drinks marketed to children; no batch / testing information.
- **Cross-check** — valid FSSAI licence + current testing compliance; reputable
  brands; a voluntary BIS mark as a bonus.
- **Evidence** — FSSAI (current water scheme), EU food law, CSE studies.

## 8. Infant formula & baby food (safety-critical)
- **What matters** — strict Codex / FSSAI compliance, contaminant limits, correct age
  stage, and that breast-milk substitutes **aren't marketed** (the **IMS Act**).
- **Check live** — FSSAI infant-food standards + Codex; contaminant limits (melamine
  history, aflatoxin M1); IMS Act promotion restrictions. **Check whether BIS
  certification is currently required for infant milk food / formula / weaning food**
  — it has been a category where BIS applies, so verify and expect it; missing
  certification is a flag.
- **Safety** — the highest-stakes food category. Be strict, prefer established
  compliant brands, and **for an infant's nutrition point to a paediatrician** — don't
  optimise on marketing or imported grey-market tins.
- **Red flags** — imported formula with non-compliant / non-English labels; "immunity
  / brain-development" claims; grey-market tins; wrong age stage; missing required
  certification.
- **Cross-check** — FSSAI-compliant (+ BIS where currently required), established
  brands, paediatric guidance.
- **Evidence** — FSSAI, Codex, WHO / IAP.

## 9. Supplements & nutraceuticals (food / drug / AYUSH boundary)
- **What matters** — **classify first**: this may be a **food / nutraceutical**
  (FSSAI's nutraceutical regime), or — if it's an ayurvedic / herbal *medicine* or
  makes disease claims — a product under the **AYUSH / drugs** framework, not FSSAI.
  Determine that before applying the food scorecard.
- **Check live** — the **current** FSSAI nutraceutical regulations (if it's a
  nutraceutical); heavy-metal & contaminant testing (see `deep-dive.md`); claim
  integrity; banned-substance risk for sports use.
- **Safety** — some **"ayurvedic / herbal" products carry documented heavy-metal (lead
  / mercury / arsenic) contamination**; watch pregnancy and drug interactions. And per
  `instructions.md`, treat **rapid-weight-loss / appetite-suppressant** framing as a
  wellbeing flag, not a normal compare.
- **Testing evidence — don't score all logos alike.** Distinguish **certification /
  testing programmes** (NSF Certified for Sport, Informed Sport, USP Verified) from
  **commercial testing / rating services** (ConsumerLab, Labdoor), and check whether
  the **exact Indian SKU / batch is actually covered**, not just a US version.
- **Red flags** — proprietary blends hiding doses; disease-treatment / cure claims
  (flag for classification); no third-party test; amino-spiking in protein.
- **Cross-check** — verified programme + Indian-SKU coverage; FSSAI-licensed (if a
  nutraceutical) or correctly AYUSH-regulated.
- **Evidence** — FSSAI, the specific certification programme, published contamination
  studies.
