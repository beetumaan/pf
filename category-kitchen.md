# Kitchen & cookware — category standards (live-checked, EU-anchored, India-strict)

> **This file is a map, not a rulebook.** Material rules, limits **and legal
> statuses change** — say **what to check and where**, don't freeze numbers or
> "banned/mandatory" statuses; pull them live.
>
> **These are non-food goods, judged on two safety lenses — classify which applies:**
> 1. **Food-contact material safety** — does anything **migrate into food**
>    (coatings, metals, plastics, glaze)?
> 2. **Product / electrical safety & performance** — is it built and wired safely,
>    and does it last?
>
> **Where to fetch current standards (durable addresses; their contents change):**
> - **EU (the anchor for material safety):** the **EU food-contact framework**
>   (general food-contact rules), the **plastics food-contact rules** (specific
>   migration limits), the **ceramics lead / cadmium limits**, the EU position on
>   **BPA in food-contact** (moving to restrict/ban — check current status +
>   transition), and the **EU PFAS restriction** under REACH (see §1). Look these up
>   live on **EUR-Lex** rather than trusting a regulation number written here.
> - **India: BIS** — certification is **mandatory only for products currently under
>   an applicable Quality Control Order / compulsory-certification requirement** (some
>   kitchen items are; many aren't). **Check the exact product against the current
>   compulsory list** — don't assume every utensil or appliance needs an ISI mark.
>   **FSSAI Packaging Regulations** apply to **food packaging, wrapping and
>   containers** (see §9), **not** to cookware generally — for cookware use the
>   applicable BIS standard, the manufacturer declaration, and migration testing.
>   **BEE star rating** = appliance energy.
> - **US FDA** food-contact cross-check; **NSF** for food-equipment certification.
>
> **Evidence hierarchy:** a **declared material / grade from a traceable
> manufacturer** + applicable certification (food-grade, ISI where currently
> mandatory) → applicable standard (EU migration limit / BIS, fetched live) →
> **independent test** (e.g. a lead / migration test) → reviews **only** for
> durability & usability. **Treat an undeclared alloy, coating or glaze as an
> evidence gap, not a pass.**
>
> *Stricter is better: where a product can meet the EU migration / material bar,
> prefer it over one that only clears the Indian minimum — but verify per material.*

**Schema per sub-category:** What matters → Check live (where) → EU-vs-India gap →
Red flags → Cross-check (marks / brands) → Review lens → Evidence.
**Generate candidates from criteria, not brand names** (cross-check, not a start).
**To add a category, copy this structure into `category-<name>.md`.**

## 1. Non-stick cookware (PTFE / "Teflon", ceramic-coat)
- **What matters** — coating chemistry, overheating behaviour, coating durability.
- **Check live** — **check the current restriction status of PFOA and PFAS** (PFOA
  has been restricted; a broad EU PFAS restriction has been **proposed and is moving
  through the REACH process** — confirm its current legal status and any
  cookware-specific scope before treating it as enacted). **Three different claims —
  don't conflate them:** **PFOA-free ≠ PFAS-free ≠ PTFE-free** (PTFE itself is a
  fluoropolymer within broad PFAS definitions; "PFOA-free" doesn't mean fluorine-free).
  "Ceramic non-stick" is a sol-gel coating, not PTFE — check what's actually claimed.
- **Safe use** — **avoid empty preheating and sustained high heat, and follow the
  manufacturer's maximum-temperature / oven-use instructions**; don't infer safety
  from a single universal temperature. No metal utensils on soft coatings.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — the EU tends to move faster on PFAS; Indian labels may say
  little → prefer brands that state their PFOA / PFAS position clearly.
- **Red flags** — no-name cheap non-stick; **flaking / scratched coating** (replace
  it); vague "non-toxic coating" with no detail; high-heat use.
- **Cross-check** — reputable brand, explicit coating claims, ISI only where it's
  actually required.
- **Review lens** — how long the coating lasts, warping.
- **Evidence** — the EU REACH / PFAS position (current), brand coating data.

## 2. Stainless steel cookware & utensils
- **What matters** — a genuinely food-suitable steel from a traceable maker — **not a
  grade number alone, and not a magnet test.**
- **Check live** — prefer a **clearly declared grade** (304 / 18-8, and 316 for higher
  corrosion resistance, are the common food-suitable austenitic grades) **from a
  traceable manufacturer**, with **appropriate corrosion resistance, sound
  construction, and applicable food-contact or BIS conformity**. Key cautions:
  - **AISI grade alone does not establish regulatory food-contact compliance.**
  - **Magnetism is *not* a food-safety test** — ferritic **430** is magnetic and can
    be legitimate in cookware components, and even worked 304 can turn slightly
    magnetic.
  - Even **304 / 316 can release some nickel and chromium** under certain conditions
    (very acidic food, long contact, a damaged surface).
  - Treat an **undeclared alloy as an evidence gap**, not a pass.
- **Suitability** — for people with significant **nickel sensitivity / allergy**, flag
  prolonged acidic cooking in stainless and suggest lower-migration options, rather
  than declaring all 304 universally best.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — EU migration limits are explicit; the Indian market carries a
  lot of unmarked-grade steel → insist on a declared grade + traceable maker.
- **Red flags** — no grade marking / untraceable maker; a persistent metallic taste;
  storing acidic food long in unknown steel.
- **Cross-check** — declared 304 / 316 from a known maker, BIS where applicable.
- **Review lens** — warping, hot-spots, pitting over time.
- **Evidence** — EU migration limits, BIS, the maker's grade certificate.

## 3. Aluminium & hard-anodized
- **What matters** — bare aluminium can **leach into acidic / salty foods**;
  hard-anodizing improves the surface but is **not** an indestructible barrier.
- **Check live** — **properly made hard-anodized cookware generally reduces direct
  aluminium exposure and improves corrosion resistance compared with worn bare
  aluminium** — but performance depends on anodizing quality, thickness, wear,
  scratches and use. **Verify the food-contact surface is intact and suitable** for
  the intended food; avoid **bare aluminium for acidic cooking / storage** (tomato,
  tamarind, curd, pickles). Don't automatically rank every hard-anodized product above
  every bare-aluminium one without product-level evidence.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — EFSA sets a tolerable weekly intake for aluminium; bare
  aluminium is very common in India → prefer anodized / stainless for acidic use.
- **Red flags** — cheap bare-aluminium pans for daily acidic cooking; pitted /
  corroded or worn-through surfaces; long-term food storage in foil / aluminium.
- **Cross-check** — hard-anodized, reputable brand, intact surface.
- **Review lens** — coating wear, even heating.
- **Evidence** — EFSA / EU food-contact, brand spec.

## 4. Cast iron & carbon steel
- **What matters** — iron transfer, seasoning, rust control.
- **Check live** — generally safe when seasoned; **it may increase iron transfer into
  food (especially with acidic or moist cooking), but the amount is unpredictable — do
  *not* treat it as a treatment for iron deficiency** or recommend it for that. Season
  well; dry and oil after washing; long acidic simmering strips seasoning and raises
  transfer. Enamelled cast iron → EU ceramics / enamel limits apply.
- **Safety** — **excess iron is undesirable for people with iron-overload disorders
  (e.g. haemochromatosis)** — flag that; don't push cast iron on them.
- **Red flags** — rusty or cracked pieces; raw unseasoned pans sold as "non-stick";
  chipped enamel.
- **Cross-check** — reputable maker, clear seasoning guidance.
- **Review lens** — smoothness, weight, rust-proneness.
- **Evidence** — food-safety consensus; enamel → EU limits.

## 5. Pressure cookers (safety-critical)
- **What matters** — this is a **pressure device**, so **safety and serviceability
  come first**, ahead of price or looks.
- **Check live** — the domestic pressure-cooker standard (**IS 2347**) has been placed
  under a compulsory BIS / Quality Control Order — **check whether it's currently
  mandatory** and, if so, that any **ISI mark is genuine** (BIS licence lookup), not
  merely printed. Then check:
  - **Safety valve + gasket** present, correct, and **easily replaceable** (spares
    actually sold);
  - a working **lid-locking / interlock** mechanism (won't open under pressure);
  - **spare-part availability** and a **service network**;
  - **recalls / reported failure (burst) patterns** for the model;
  - the body material (apply §2 stainless / §3 aluminium suitability).
- **Red flags** — no genuine ISI mark where one is required; no spare gasket / safety
  valve available; a damaged or very old unit; an unknown brand with no service or
  spares; a model with a recall / burst history.
- **Cross-check** — a verified BIS licence for IS 2347, reputable brand, confirmed
  spare parts + service.
- **Review lens** — long-term sealing, valve reliability, spare availability.
- **Evidence** — BIS (IS 2347 / current QCO status), brand service info, recall /
  incident reports.

## 6. Plastic food storage & containers
- **What matters** — genuine **food-contact authorisation**, **BPA / phthalates**,
  heat / microwave suitability — **not the recycling triangle.**
- **Check live** — **recycling codes identify the resin family; they are *not* safety
  certifications**: code 7 is an "other" catch-all, and a "5" is **not** automatically
  microwave-safe. Instead require **intended food-contact use, temperature
  instructions, microwave / dishwasher markings where relevant, traceability and
  compliance documentation**; the **EU food-contact plastics rules** set specific
  migration limits, and the **EU has moved to restrict / ban BPA in food-contact** —
  **check the current EU status and transition** rather than asserting it. Note:
  **FSSAI has been developing a framework for approved food-contact recycled PET
  (rPET)** — **check its current status**; don't treat "recycled" as automatically
  unsafe.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — EU sets explicit migration limits and is tightening BPA;
  Indian labels are often silent → prefer food-grade + BPA-free + clear use
  instructions.
- **Red flags** — no food-grade / food-contact marking; **heating plastic not marked
  microwave-safe**; storing hot, oily or acidic food in cheap plastic; **reusing
  single-use bottles**.
- **Cross-check** — stated food-contact use + temperature marks + reputable brand.
- **Review lens** — staining, odour retention, warping.
- **Evidence** — EU food-contact plastics rules (current), FSSAI packaging framework,
  brand documentation.

## 7. Melamine & ceramic / glazed tableware
- **What matters** — melamine is **fine as labelled tableware** (including many
  ordinary warm foods) but releases formaldehyde / melamine **if misused with high
  heat**; **ceramic glaze** can leach **lead / cadmium**, especially cheap, imported,
  hand-glazed or decorative pieces.
- **Check live** — **use melamine only as labelled tableware: do NOT microwave, cook
  in it, or exceed the manufacturer's stated temperature range**, and avoid damaged /
  heavily scratched / untraceable melamine, particularly with very hot acidic foods.
  For ceramics, check the **current EU ceramics lead / cadmium limits** and look for
  food-safe / lead-free glaze certification.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — the EU enforces explicit ceramic lead / cadmium limits; cheap
  glazed and decorative pottery in India may be untested → treat unbranded bright glaze
  with caution.
- **Red flags** — melamine used for microwaving / cooking; brightly glazed cheap /
  unbranded ceramic used for food; **decorative pottery repurposed as dinnerware.**
- **Cross-check** — food-safe / lead-free glaze certification, reputable brand.
- **Review lens** — crazing / cracking of glaze, staining.
- **Evidence** — EU ceramics lead/cadmium limits (current), independent lead tests.

## 8. Small kitchen appliances (mixer, kettle, induction, toaster, air-fryer)
- **What matters** — **electrical safety**, **food-contact parts** (jar / bowl
  material, BPA-free), build quality, energy.
- **Check live** — check whether the **exact electrical product is currently under a
  mandatory BIS requirement** and, if so, that a genuine ISI mark is present;
  food-contact parts should be **food-grade**. **A CE mark is not a substitute** — CE
  relates to EU electrical conformity, does **not** prove Indian legal compliance, and
  does **not** establish food-contact safety.
- **EU-vs-India gap** — imports may carry CE but still need any mandatory BIS for
  India; don't treat a foreign mark as Indian compliance.
- **Red flags** — missing a *required* ISI mark; no-name imports; dodgy wiring / no
  earthing; non-food-grade jars; no warranty / service.
- **Cross-check** — mandatory BIS where it currently applies, reputable brand, warranty
  & service network.
- **Review lens** — motor longevity, jar sealing, safety cut-offs, noise.
- **Evidence** — BIS (where mandatory), brand certification.
- *Larger appliances (fridge, microwave, dishwasher) and their **BEE energy rating** →
  a future `category-electronics.md`.*

## 9. Storage & wrapping materials (foil, cling film, paper)
- **What matters** — food-contact compliance of what actually touches food (this is
  where **FSSAI Packaging Regulations** squarely apply).
- **Check live** — **FSSAI Packaging Regulations**: **no newspaper / printed paper for
  food** (ink migration — common but unsafe), recycled-plastic limits (an rPET
  framework is developing — check current); aluminium **foil** (avoid prolonged
  contact with acidic / salty food); **cling film** heat and food-type limits (only if
  rated).
- **EU-vs-India gap** — EU food-contact rules are explicit and enforced; the Indian
  norm of newspaper wrapping is a real, avoidable exposure.
- **Red flags** — **newspaper- / printed-paper-wrapped food**; unrated coloured
  plastic bags for food; microwaving cling film not rated for it; foil with long-term
  acidic contact.
- **Cross-check** — food-grade marked, FSSAI-compliant.
- **Review lens** — n/a (safety-led).
- **Evidence** — FSSAI packaging regs, EU food-contact.
